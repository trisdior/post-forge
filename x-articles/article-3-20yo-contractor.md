# What It's Really Like to Own a Construction Company at 20

**I'm typing this at 11 PM with drywall dust still in my hair.**

Tomorrow I have two jobs scheduled. One estimate call. Three follow-ups to send. And somewhere in there, I need to figure out why my truck is making that weird sound again.

I'm 20 years old. I own Valencia Construction. And I'm going to tell you what this life is actually like.

Not the highlight reel. Not the motivational fluff. The real shit.

Let's get this out of the way: **I didn't inherit a business.** I didn't have rich parents. I didn't get a loan. I didn't have a trust fund.

I started Valencia Construction with:

- Basic tools

**And that's exactly how I wanted it.**

## Why Construction?

People always ask me this. "Why construction? You're young. You could do tech. You could do content. You could do anything."

Here's why I chose construction:

**1. It Can't Be Outsourced**

You can't send a fence installation to India. You can't automate a bathroom renovation with robots (not yet anyway). The work has to happen in person, on site, with human hands.

That means there will ALWAYS be demand.

**2. It's Real Money**

I'm not chasing likes and hoping for sponsorships. I'm not waiting for my content to "take off." I finish a job, I send an invoice, I get paid.

Cash flow is king. Construction has it.

**3. The Skills Are Forever**

If the economy crashes tomorrow, people will still need their houses fixed. The skills I'm building—carpentry, project management, client relations—will never be worthless.

**4. The Competition Is Weak**

This is the dirty secret of the trades: **most contractors are terrible at business.**

They're great at their craft. They can build anything. But they can't:
- Answer the phone on time
- Send professional estimates
- Communicate with clients
- Show up when they say they will
- Market themselves at all

I watched contractors lose jobs because they took 5 days to return a call. I watched customers beg for someone reliable.

**I realized that if I just did the basics—answer the phone, send estimates quickly, communicate clearly, show up on time—I would win.** Not because I'm the most skilled (I'm still learning). But because the bar is underground.

## The Grind Is Real

Let me walk you through a typical week:

**Monday:**
- 6:30 AM: Wake up, check Steve's morning brief (my AI ops manager)
- 7:00 AM: Load the truck, grab breakfast
- 8:00 AM - 5:00 PM: On site working
- 6:00 PM: Home, shower, eat
- 7:00 PM - 10:00 PM: Send estimates, schedule next week, respond to leads, update project tracking
- 11:00 PM: Maybe some personal time. Maybe just crash.

**Repeat Tuesday through Saturday.**

Sunday? That's when I catch up on everything I didn't finish during the week. And maybe do laundry.

**There's no weekend when you're building something from nothing.**

I'm not complaining. I chose this. But I want you to understand: this isn't a lifestyle business (yet). This is grind mode. This is "build the foundation now so you can relax later."

## Lessons I've Learned Building From Scratch

**1. Speed Wins Everything**

The contractor who responds fastest usually gets the job. Not the cheapest. Not the most experienced. The fastest.

When a lead comes in, I respond within the hour. Sometimes within minutes (thanks to Hunter alerting me).

Most contractors respond in 2-3 days. By then, I've already sent the estimate and scheduled the job.

**Speed is a competitive advantage that costs nothing.**

**2. Communication Is 50% of the Job**

Clients don't know how to evaluate your work. They can't tell if your framing is professional-grade or amateur.

But they CAN tell if you're communicating clearly.

- Did you explain what you're doing?
- Did you give them updates?
- Did you show up when you said you would?
- Did you respond to their texts?

**Most complaints about contractors aren't about quality. They're about communication.**

I over-communicate on every job. Daily updates. Photos. Clear timelines. And my clients love me for it.

**3. The Basics Beat Everything**

Fancy marketing doesn't matter if you don't answer the phone.

Slick branding doesn't matter if your estimate is late.

A nice truck doesn't matter if you show up three hours late.

**Master the basics first.** Everything else is noise.

**4. Energy Management Is More Important Than Time Management**

You can have 24 hours available and get nothing done if you're exhausted.

I've learned that my most important hours are 7-10 PM when I'm doing admin work. If I'm too tired, that work doesn't happen, and my business suffers.

So I protect my energy:
- No alcohol (wastes the next day)
- Sleep is non-negotiable
- I batch similar tasks together
- I automate everything I can

**Your energy is your most valuable resource. Treat it that way.**

**5. No One Is Coming to Save You**

This is the hardest lesson.

When you're an employee, someone else worries about payroll. Someone else finds the clients. Someone else handles the problems.

When you're the owner, **there is no one else.**

Lead doesn't come in? Your problem.
Client is upset? Your problem.
Equipment breaks? Your problem.
Cash flow is tight? Your problem.

**No one is coming to save you.**

The sooner you accept this, the sooner you stop waiting for luck and start creating it.

## The Old-School + New-School Combo

Here's what I think most young entrepreneurs miss:

**You need both the old-school hustle AND the new-school tech.**

Old-school alone? You're working too hard, leaving money on the table, getting outcompeted by people like me.

New-school alone? You're living in spreadsheets and apps but you've never actually done the work. You have no real skills. You can't close a deal or swing a hammer.

**The combination is where the magic happens:**

- Real construction skills (old school) + AI automation (new school)
- In-person client relationships (old school) + Digital marketing (new school)  
- Hard work ethic (old school) + Systems thinking (new school)
- Learning from experienced tradesmen (old school) + Building my own tools (new school)

I'm on job sites learning from guys who have done this for decades. Then I go home and build AI systems they've never heard of.

**That's the edge.**

## For the Other Young Entrepreneurs

If you're reading this and you're 18, 19, 20, 21... still figuring out your path... here's what I want you to know:

**1. You Have More Time Than You Think**

I'm 20. I have 45+ years of working life ahead of me. Even if I don't hit my 2026 goals, I have DECADES to figure it out.

You're not behind. You're early. Use that.

**2. Pick Something Real**

Everyone wants to be an influencer or a crypto trader or a dropshipper.

Meanwhile, there's a massive shortage of electricians, plumbers, HVAC techs, and contractors. Guys in these trades are retiring and nobody's replacing them.

**Real skills = real money.** Pick something you can actually DO, not just talk about.

**3. Start Before You're Ready**

I didn't feel ready to start a construction company. I was terrified. I didn't know what I was doing.

I started anyway.

**You will never feel ready.** Start now. Figure it out as you go.

**4. Sacrifice Now, Live Later**

I don't party. I don't go out much. I don't spend money on dumb shit.

I'm sacrificing my early 20s so my late 20s and 30s are incredible.

Most people do the opposite—live it up now and struggle later. Your choice.

**5. Ignore Everyone Who Doubts You**

When I told people I was starting a construction company at 20, some laughed. Some said I was too young. Some said I should "get more experience first."

I stopped talking and started doing.

**Results shut everyone up.** Focus on results.
