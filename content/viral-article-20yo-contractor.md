# I'm 20 and Own a Construction Company. Here's What Nobody Tells You.

*By Tris Valencia, Owner of Valencia Construction — Chicago, IL*

---

I turned 20 last year. Most people my age are in college, working part-time jobs, or figuring out what they want to do with their lives.

I'm running a licensed and insured general contracting company in one of the biggest cities in America.

And no, I didn't inherit it. No family money. No connections in the trades. I built Valencia Construction from zero — and I'm still building it.

Here's what I've learned so far.

---

## Everyone Thinks You're Too Young

The first time I walked into a supply house to open a contractor account, the guy behind the counter looked at me like I was lost. "You sure you're in the right place?"

I get it. Construction is an industry where experience is everything. Homeowners want gray hair and a weathered truck. When a 20-year-old shows up to give an estimate, there's a moment — you can see it in their eyes — where they're calculating whether to take you seriously.

Here's what I've learned: the doubt goes away the second you demonstrate competence. Know your numbers. Know your materials. Show up when you say you will. Call back within the hour. Do those four things and you're already ahead of half the contractors in Chicago.

## I Started Because I Saw the Problem

Before I started Valencia Construction, I worked with other contractors. And I kept seeing the same complaints from homeowners:

- "My contractor ghosted me."
- "They said $15K, now it's $28K."
- "I can't get anyone to call me back."
- "They started and never came back to finish."

This isn't a skills problem. Most contractors do good work. It's a **communication** problem. It's a **business** problem. And I realized — if I can just be reliable, transparent, and actually answer my phone, I'd have a real competitive advantage.

That's the bar. I know it sounds low. But in this industry? It's everything.

## What I Actually Do

Valencia Construction handles everything from small handyman jobs to full kitchen and bathroom remodels. We do flooring, tile, drywall, painting, plumbing, electrical, cabinet work — if it's in a home, we probably do it.

But I'm not trying to be the biggest company. I'm trying to be the **best experience**. When you hire us:

- **You talk to me.** Not a salesperson. Not a project manager who's juggling 30 jobs. Me — the owner.
- **The price is the price.** I give detailed estimates with line items. No mystery charges.
- **We show up.** On time. Every time. If something changes, I call you before you have to call me.

That's it. That's the whole philosophy. Be a normal, competent, communicative human being, and you'll stand out in this industry.

## The Hard Parts Nobody Talks About

### Cash flow will keep you up at night
Materials cost money upfront. Labor costs money every week. Clients pay when the job is done — or sometimes 30 days after. That gap? That's where businesses die. I've had weeks where I was spending every dollar I had, waiting on a check, doing the math at 2 AM. It gets tight. Real tight.

### Insurance and licensing aren't optional
Some guys work without insurance to keep prices low. I get it — insurance is expensive. But one accident, one lawsuit, one damaged property, and you're done. Not just your business — your personal finances, everything. I pay for full coverage because I'm building something that lasts. Shortcuts are for people who don't plan to be around next year.

### You'll do every job nobody wants
When you're starting out, you don't get to be picky. Leaky faucet at 7 PM? I'm there. Patch drywall in a basement for $200? On my way. You take the small jobs because they lead to big jobs, and every single client is a potential referral. I've gotten $20K remodel jobs from $300 handyman calls.

### Loneliness is real
My friends are in college. They're going to parties and worrying about midterms. I'm calculating material costs and scheduling subcontractors. I don't say this for sympathy — I chose this. But nobody tells you how isolating it is to be a 20-year-old business owner. Most people my age don't understand what I'm doing or why.

## Why I'm Doing This

Because I want to build something real.

Not an app. Not a dropshipping store. Not some online thing that could disappear tomorrow. A real business, in a real industry, that makes real things for real people.

Construction isn't going anywhere. People will always need homes built and renovated. AI can't swing a hammer. Robots can't tile a shower. This industry rewards people who show up and do good work — and that's exactly the kind of game I want to play.

My goal? Build Valencia Construction into one of the most trusted names in Chicago remodeling. Not overnight. Brick by brick — literally.

## What I'd Tell Other Young People

If you're my age and thinking about starting a business — especially in the trades — here's my honest advice:

1. **Start before you're ready.** You'll never feel ready. Get your license, get your insurance, and start.
2. **Answer your phone.** Seriously. This alone will get you business.
3. **Underpromise and overdeliver.** Every time.
4. **Take the small jobs.** They're how you build a reputation.
5. **Save more than you think you need.** Cash flow problems kill more businesses than bad work.
6. **Document everything.** Every conversation, every estimate, every change order. In writing.
7. **Don't compete on price.** Compete on reliability. The cheapest contractor is almost never the best experience.
8. **Be patient.** This takes years. Not months. Years.
