By Tris Valencia, Owner of Valencia Construction

There's this look people give me when I hand them my business card. They glance at it, then glance at me, and I can see the math happening behind their eyes. This kid owns a construction company?

Yeah. I do. I'm 20 years old, and I run Valencia Construction — a licensed and insured general contracting company in Chicago. Kitchen remodels, bathroom renovations, flooring, tile, plumbing, electrical, drywall, painting, handyman work. The whole thing.

And before you ask — no, my dad didn't hand it to me. No, I didn't win the lottery. No, there's no secret trust fund. I built this from nothing. Literally nothing.

This is the story of how that happened, what it's really like, and what I've learned so far.

Why I Started

I didn't grow up dreaming about construction. Nobody does, really. You don't see little kids saying "I want to install subway tile when I grow up."

But I grew up around it. I was always the kid who wanted to know how things worked — why this wall could come down but that one couldn't, how water actually gets from the street to your faucet, why some houses feel solid and others feel like they're held together with hope.

By the time I was in my late teens, I was working with contractors, picking up skills, watching how the business side worked. And here's what I noticed:

Most of it was broken.

Contractors who don't answer their phones. Guys who take your money and disappear for three weeks. Projects that were quoted at $15K and end up at $25K because nobody wrote anything down. Homeowners getting burned over and over again.

I kept thinking: This isn't that complicated. Show up. Do what you said. Charge what you quoted. Answer your phone.

So I started doing exactly that. And it turns out, that's enough to build a company.

The Beginning Was Ugly

I'm not going to romanticize this. The beginning sucked.

I had no website. No business cards. No truck with a logo on it. I had a phone number, a set of tools, and a willingness to work harder than anyone else in the room.

My first jobs came from Craigslist posts and Facebook groups. Small stuff — patching drywall, painting apartments, fixing leaky faucets. The kind of work that established contractors won't even return a call for.

But I showed up. On time. Every time. And I did the work right.

One landlord told his friend. That friend had a bigger job. That bigger job led to a referral. Then another one. Then another.

That's how it works. Not some overnight success story. Not a viral TikTok. Just showing up, doing good work, and letting the results speak.

What It's Really Like

Here's the part nobody tells you about owning a business at 20.

The loneliness. Most of my friends from high school are in college right now. They're going to parties, sleeping until noon, figuring out what they want to be. Meanwhile, I'm up at 5 AM pricing out materials for a kitchen demo, answering emails at midnight, and spending my weekends writing proposals instead of going out.

I'm not complaining — I chose this. But anyone who tells you entrepreneurship is glamorous is selling something.

The doubt. There are nights where I lie in bed and think: Am I crazy? Who starts a construction company at 20? What if I fail? What if I mess up someone's home?

That doubt doesn't go away. You just learn to work through it. You learn that doubt is the tax you pay for doing something ambitious.

The money stress. When you're starting out, cash flow is a rollercoaster. You might have $20K worth of jobs lined up but $47 in your checking account because materials needed to be bought upfront. You learn to budget differently. You learn that revenue isn't profit, and profit isn't cash in your pocket.

But then there are the wins.

The first time a client shook my hand after a kitchen remodel and said, "This is better than I imagined" — I almost teared up. No joke. That feeling? That's why I do this.

The first time someone referred me without me asking. The first time I had to turn down a job because my schedule was full. The first time I looked at my bank account and realized I was actually making a living doing something I built from scratch.

Those moments make all the 5 AM mornings and midnight emails worth it.

What Makes Valencia Different

I know every contractor says they're different. So let me be specific.

I answer my phone. Not a secretary. Not a voicemail that never gets returned. Me. If I'm on a job and can't pick up, I call back within the hour. Every single time. I know that sounds basic, but ask anyone who's tried to hire a contractor in Chicago — getting someone to return a call is like winning the lottery.

I show up when I say I will. If I say Tuesday at 9 AM, I'm there Tuesday at 9 AM. Not Tuesday afternoon. Not "sometime this week." Tuesday at 9 AM. This shouldn't be revolutionary, but here we are.

Everything is in writing. Before any work starts, you get a detailed scope of work, a clear price, and a timeline. No surprises. No "oh, that's going to be extra." If something changes, we talk about it and put the change in writing too.

I don't ask for full payment upfront. 50% deposit, 25% at the midpoint, 25% when you're happy with the finished product. Your money is protected because I have to earn it as we go.

Owner-operated means owner-accountable. When you hire a big company, you talk to a salesman, then a project manager, then a foreman, and somehow your vision gets lost in translation. When you hire me, you talk to me. I'm the estimate, the plan, the execution, and the quality control. One person. No game of telephone.

What I've Learned (Advice for Young People)

If you're my age and thinking about starting a business — any business, not just construction — here's what I wish someone had told me:

1. You don't need to have it all figured out.

I didn't have a 50-page business plan. I didn't take a course on entrepreneurship. I had a skill, a work ethic, and the willingness to figure it out as I went. That's enough to start. You can refine everything else along the way.

2. Your reputation is everything.

Especially when you're young. People are already skeptical. Every single interaction is an audition. Answer every message. Be early. Overdeliver. One bad review when you're starting out can sink you. One great experience can set you up for months.

3. Learn to be uncomfortable.

The first time I quoted a $20K job, my hands were shaking. The first time I had a difficult conversation with a client, I wanted to throw up. The first time I had to fire a subcontractor, I barely slept the night before. But every uncomfortable thing I did made the next one easier. Growth lives on the other side of discomfort.

4. Nobody is coming to save you.

There's no safety net. No boss to blame. No corporate to escalate to. If something goes wrong, it's on you to fix it. That's terrifying, but it's also the most empowering thing in the world. You learn to trust yourself because you have no other choice.

5. Age is an advantage, not a disadvantage.

Yeah, some people look at me sideways because I'm 20. But you know what? I have more energy, more hunger, and more to prove than the contractor who's been doing this for 30 years and is just going through the motions. I'm not coasting. I'm climbing. Clients who understand that? They get the best version of me.

6. Start before you're ready.

You'll never feel ready. You'll never have enough money, enough experience, enough confidence. Start anyway. The experience, money, and confidence come from doing the work — not from waiting until you feel prepared.

Why I'm Writing This

Honestly? I'm writing this because I want people to know that Valencia Construction isn't just another contractor.

It's me. My name. My reputation. My future.

Every tile I set, every wall I paint, every kitchen I remodel — that's me telling the world what I'm capable of. I don't take that lightly.

I'm also writing this because when I was 17 and thinking about starting a business, I would have killed to read something like this. Something real. Not a LinkedIn post from a CEO worth $50 million telling me to "follow my passion." Something from someone in the trenches, still figuring it out, still grinding, still scared sometimes — but doing it anyway.

If that's you, keep going. It's worth it.
